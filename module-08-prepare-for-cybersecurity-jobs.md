## Events, Incidents, and Data Protection

- **Event:** Any observable activity in a system or network.
- **Security event:** Activity that may have security relevance.
- **Security incident:** Event that may harm confidentiality, integrity, or availability.
- **Security mindset:** Habit of thinking about risk, impact, evidence, and responsible action.

Data sensitivity examples:

- **Public data:** Safe to share openly.
- **Private data:** Should be limited to authorized use.
- **Sensitive data:** Could cause harm if exposed.
- **Confidential data:** Restricted business or personal information.

## Escalation

- **Escalation:** Passing an issue to the right person or team when it needs more authority, skill, or urgency.
- **Escalation policy:** Defines when, how, and to whom incidents should be escalated.

Escalate when:

- Malware is suspected.
- Unauthorized access is detected.
- Sensitive data may be exposed.
- A system is unavailable.
- The impact is unclear but potentially serious.
- The response requires approval or higher privilege.

## Stakeholders

- **Stakeholder:** Person or group affected by security decisions or incident outcomes.

Common stakeholders:

- SOC analysts
- IT administrators
- Risk managers
- Operations managers
- Legal and compliance teams
- Data owners
- Executives such as CISO, CIO, CFO, or CEO

## Communication

Good cybersecurity communication is:

- Clear
- Accurate
- Timely
- Evidence-based
- Appropriate for the audience
- Focused on risk and action

For technical teams, include details such as logs, systems, timestamps, indicators, and steps taken. For non-technical stakeholders, explain impact, business risk, and recommended action.

## Dashboards and Visuals

Dashboards help summarize security information quickly.

Useful dashboard items:

- Alert counts
- Incident status
- Severity levels
- Affected assets
- Trend over time
- Response progress
- Risk or control status

## Reliable Sources and Community

Reliable sources help security learners stay current.

Examples:

- Vendor security advisories
- CISA alerts
- NIST publications
- OWASP resources
- MITRE ATT&CK
- Product documentation
- Professional security communities

## Job Preparation Topics

Study and practice explaining:

- CIA triad
- Threat, vulnerability, and risk
- Phishing indicators
- SIEM purpose
- Alert triage steps
- Incident response lifecycle
- Least privilege
- Logs and evidence
- Linux and SQL basics
- Python automation basics

## STAR Method

Use STAR to answer behavioral interview questions:

- **Situation:** What was happening?
- **Task:** What needed to be done?
- **Action:** What did you do?
- **Result:** What happened after your action?

## Elevator Pitch

A short introduction should include:

- Current cybersecurity learning focus
- Relevant IT or technical background
- Key strengths
- Type of role or work being pursued
- One clear reason the field interests you

## Quick Review

- Security work depends on communication and escalation.
- Incidents should be documented clearly and routed to the right people.
- Dashboards help summarize risk and response status.
- Reliable sources and communities support continuous learning.
- Interview preparation should connect concepts to practical examples.
